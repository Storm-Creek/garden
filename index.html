🌼 Companion Planting — Ed's 3-Layer System

🌿 Layer 1: Smell Camouflage

Confuse pests & mask crop scent

  • French marigolds — interplant in EVERY food bed
  • Basil — with tomatoes & peppers (repels hornworms, aphids, whiteflies)
  • Cilantro — in herb bed and through greens beds
  • Summer savory — with beans (repels bean beetles)

🎯 Layer 2: Trap Crops

Sacrifice plants that lure pests AWAY from food

  • Nasturtiums — bed edges & corners; aphids eat these instead
  • Radishes — with squash/cukes; flea beetles prefer them
  • Blue Hubbard squash — 1-2 on mound 18-1; squash bugs LOVE it. Check daily, hand-pick.

🐞 Layer 3: Beneficial Insect Border

Attract predators that eat pests

  • Fennel — garden border (parasitic wasps, ladybugs, hoverflies)
  • Dill — border & bed 2 (lacewings, ladybugs)
  • Sweet alyssum — low border edging ALL beds (hoverfly larvae eat aphids)
  • Yarrow — garden border (ladybugs, parasitic wasps, hoverflies)

✅ Good Pairs & ❌ Avoid

Tomato + Basil — repels hornworms, improves flavor
Tomato + Marigold — repels aphids & whiteflies
Pepper + Basil — repels aphids, thrips
Cucumber + Dill — attracts predators
Strawberry + Borage — pollinators, improves yield
Onion + Chamomile — improves flavor
Onion + Carrot — repels carrot fly/onion fly
Squash + Nasturtium — trap aphids
Beans + Savory — repels bean beetles
Tomato + Fennel — fennel inhibits tomato growth
Beans + Onion — onions stunt bean growth
Dill + Carrot — can cross-pollinate, odd flavors
Cucumber + Sage — sage inhibits cucumber growth
Tomato + Brassicas — compete for calcium
💩 Fertilizer Quick Reference

🪵 Wood Ashes

0.5 oz / SF

Raises pH, adds potassium + calcium. Great for tomatoes & peppers.

⚠ NEVER near blueberries!

🐔 Chicken Manure

1 lb / SF (composted)

Hot — must be aged 3+ months. For heavy feeders. Apply in spring prep.

🪵 Wood Chips

4-6" mulch layer

Moisture retention, weed suppression, slow nutrient release. Use everywhere.

⚖ 10-10-10

Side-dress mid-season

Balanced fertilizer for heavy feeders if organic isn't enough.

🥚 Eggshells

Handful per planting hole

Calcium for tomatoes (prevents blossom end rot). Crush & put in hole at transplant.

🌲 Pine Needles

2-4" mulch layer

Acidifies soil for blueberries. Use as mulch around acid-loving plants.

Spring Bed Amendment Guide — Per Bed

Beds 1–10 are 4×12 ft (48 SF). Light top-dress — these are established raised beds, not new builds. Scatter & rake in, then mulch after planting.

Application order: 1) Chicken poop — scatter & work in. 2) Wood ashes — scatter & rake in. 3) General compost — spread & work into top few inches. 4) Veggie compost — top layer, work in lightly. 5) Mulch — on top AFTER planting, keep 2” away from stems.

Bed-by-Bed Amendment Checklist

Work through one bed at a time. For each bed: scatter chicken poop & ashes, work in. Spread composts, work in. Mulch on top after planting. Check off as you go.

Quick measures: Ashes = handfuls (dust visible on soil). Chicken poop = 1 rounded shovel ≈ 5 lbs. Compost = 1 shovel ≈ 8 lbs, 1 bucket ≈ 25 lbs. Mulch = 1 shovel ≈ 5 lbs, 1 wheelbarrow ≈ 50–60 lbs.

Seasonal Feeding Schedule — Mid-Season Boosts

Many plants need feeding beyond the spring amendment. Here’s what to feed and when throughout the season. Chicken manure tea = 1 lb composted poop soaked in 5 gal water for 2–3 days, dilute to tea color.

May — First Feeding

  • Bed 1 (Tomatoes & Peppers): Side-dress 2 lbs chicken poop or 1 cup 10-10-10 per plant once fruit sets
  • Bed 13 (Blackberries): 3 lbs chicken poop along row at bloom time. Blackberries are heavy feeders during fruiting.
  • Bed 18-1 (Squash): Side-dress 2 lbs chicken poop when vines start running
  • Bed 6 (Strawberries): Light chicken manure tea after first harvest flush
  • Bed 19 (Blueberries): 1 lb chicken poop or acidic fertilizer (Holly-tone) around drip line at bloom

June — Peak Growth Boost

  • Bed 1 (Tomatoes & Peppers): Chicken manure tea every 2 weeks through harvest. These never stop being hungry.
  • Bed 1+ (Cucumbers): Side-dress 1 lb chicken poop or manure tea when fruiting starts
  • Bed 11 (Catawba Grapes): 2 lbs chicken poop around vines after fruit set
  • Bed 15 (Scuppernong): 2 lbs chicken poop around vines after fruit set
  • Bed 20 (Melons & Pumpkins): Side-dress 3 lbs chicken poop when vines are running & fruiting
  • Bed 3 (Chard): Light chicken manure tea if chard leaves look pale/small

July — Mid-Season Push

  • Bed 1 (Tomatoes & Peppers): Continue manure tea every 2 weeks. Side-dress 1 cup 10-10-10 if plants look tired.
  • Bed 13 (Blackberries): 2 lbs chicken poop after first harvest wave. Feeds the second flush.
  • Bed 18-1 (Squash): Manure tea every 2 weeks if still producing
  • Bed 20 (Melons & Pumpkins): Manure tea every 2 weeks. Stop feeding pumpkins once fruit reaches full size.
  • Bed 9 (Flowers): Light manure tea if zinnias/sunflowers slow down

August — Late Season & Fall Prep

  • Bed 1 (Tomatoes & Peppers): Last manure tea feeding. Stop by late Aug — let plants wind down.
  • Bed 13 (Blackberries): 2 lbs chicken poop after final harvest. Feeds next year’s cane growth.
  • Bed 4 (Arugula): Light compost top-dress before fall succession sowing
  • Bed 3 (Fall greens): Light compost top-dress before fall planting
  • Bed 17 (Pears): STOP feeding — no fertilizer after Aug. Trees need to harden off for winter.

Oct–Nov — End of Season

  • Bed 13 (Blackberries): 5 lbs compost + 20 lbs mulch after pruning. Sets up next year.
  • Bed 16 (Asparagus): 3 lbs chicken poop + top up mulch to 4–6” after ferns die back
  • Bed 19 (Blueberries): Top up pine needle mulch. No other fertilizer until spring.
  • Bed 11 & 15 (Grapes): 5 lbs compost around base after leaf drop
  • All raised beds: Spread 5 lbs compost per bed as fall top-dress, let winter rains work it in

Quick rule of thumb: Heavy feeders (tomatoes, peppers, squash, melons, blackberries) get fed every 2–3 weeks during active fruiting. Light feeders (herbs, arugula, onions) just need the spring amendment. Perennials (grapes, pears, blueberries, asparagus) get fed 2–3 times total: spring, after fruit set, and fall.

🧪 Natural Sprays & Pest Control

No RoundUp, no synthetic pesticides. Everything here is food-safe and won't kill beneficial insects when used correctly. Always spray in the MORNING or EVENING — never midday sun.

🌿 Neem Oil Spray

For: Aphids, whiteflies, Japanese beetles, squash bugs, fungal diseases (mildew, blight)

Recipe (1 gallon):
• 1 gallon warm water
• 2 tablespoons cold-pressed neem oil
• 1 teaspoon liquid dish soap (emulsifier)

Mix: Combine soap + warm water first, then add neem oil. Shake well. Use within 8 hours — neem breaks down fast.

Apply: Spray all leaf surfaces (top AND bottom) every 7-10 days. Evening is best — neem degrades in sunlight. Reapply after rain.

⚠ Don't spray on flowers when bees are active. Spray evening after bees are done foraging.

🧄 Garlic & Hot Pepper Spray

For: Aphids, cabbage worms, flea beetles, Japanese beetles, deer/rabbit deterrent

Recipe (1 gallon):
• 1 gallon water
• 1 whole head garlic, minced or blended
• 3-4 hot peppers (cayenne, habanero) OR 2 tbsp cayenne powder
• 1 tablespoon liquid dish soap
• 1 tablespoon vegetable oil (sticking agent)

Mix: Blend garlic + peppers in 2 cups water. Let steep overnight (stronger). Strain through cheesecloth. Add soap + oil + remaining water.

Apply: Spray directly on pests and on leaf surfaces. Reapply every 5-7 days and after rain. Works by smell and taste — bugs and animals hate it.

⚠ Wear gloves when handling hot peppers. Don't touch your eyes. Wash harvested produce.

🧴 Insecticidal Soap Spray

For: Soft-bodied insects — aphids, whiteflies, spider mites, mealybugs

Recipe (1 quart spray bottle):
• 1 quart water
• 1 tablespoon pure castile soap (Dr. Bronner's unscented)

That's it. Shake gently.

Apply: Spray directly ON the bugs — must make contact to work. Suffocates soft-bodied insects. Won't harm hard-shelled beetles. Reapply every 3-5 days until gone.

⚠ Don't use dish soap with degreasers/antibacterial (Dawn Ultra, etc). Use pure castile only. Test on one leaf first — some plants are sensitive.

⚔ Vinegar Weed Killer (Herbicide)

For: Weeds in paths, edges, gravel areas. RoundUp replacement.

Recipe (1 gallon):
• 1 gallon white vinegar (5% grocery store is OK, 20% horticultural is stronger)
• 1 cup salt (table salt or rock salt)
• 1 tablespoon liquid dish soap (helps it stick)

Mix: Dissolve salt in vinegar, add soap. Use a dedicated sprayer (label it — don't mix with plant sprayers).

Apply: Spray directly on weeds on a hot sunny day. The sun activates the burn. Works best on young weeds. May need 2-3 applications for tough perennial weeds.

⚠ NON-SELECTIVE — kills anything green it touches! Keep away from crop plants. Salt sterilizes soil, so only use on paths/gravel where you don't want anything to grow. Do NOT use in garden beds.

🍄 Baking Soda Fungicide

For: Powdery mildew, early blight, black spot, leaf spot

Recipe (1 gallon):
• 1 gallon water
• 1 tablespoon baking soda
• 1 tablespoon vegetable oil or neem oil
• ½ teaspoon liquid dish soap

Mix: Dissolve baking soda in water, add oil and soap.

Apply: Spray on leaves weekly as PREVENTIVE (before disease shows). Once fungus is established, it slows spread but won't cure. Best for tomatoes, cucumbers, squash, grapes.

Tip: For tomato blight, combine with good air circulation (don't overcrowd Bed 1) and morning watering at root level (never wet the leaves).

🏜 Diatomaceous Earth (DE)

For: Slugs, ants, earwigs, flea beetles, crawling insects

No mixing needed — dust directly from the bag.

• Buy food-grade DE only (NOT pool-grade)
• Dust lightly around plant bases and on soil surface
• Reapply after rain or watering

How it works: Microscopic sharp edges cut through insect exoskeletons. They dehydrate and die. Harmless to humans, pets, and earthworms (they're protected by slime).

⚠ Don't apply on flowers — can harm bees. Wear a dust mask when applying (irritates lungs if inhaled). Only effective when dry.

🍄 Mushroom Integration — NEW 2026

Why Mushrooms in the Garden?

Mushrooms break down wood chips and organic matter into plant-available nutrients, improve soil structure, and suppress some plant pathogens. Wine caps in particular are a perfect garden companion — they fruit in mulched beds you're already maintaining. No extra space needed.

🍄 Wine Cap (King Stropharia)

Location: IN your existing wood chip mulch paths & around raised beds

Shade needed: Partial shade to dappled sun — mulched paths between beds work great

Substrate: Fresh hardwood chips (you already use these!). 4-6" layer.

When to inoculate: March-April, after last hard freeze

Fruits: Late summer through fall (Aug-Oct). Large burgundy caps, excellent eating.

How: Mix sawdust spawn into a 4-6" layer of fresh wood chips. Keep moist. That's it.

🍄 Shiitake (Log-Grown)

Location: Under pear trees (Bed 17) — perfect dappled shade

Shade needed: YES — 80% shade ideal. Under the pear canopy is perfect.

Substrate: Fresh-cut hardwood logs (oak, sweetgum, maple) 3-6" diameter, 3-4 ft long

When to inoculate: Feb-April (within 6 weeks of cutting logs)

Fruits: 6-18 months after plugging, then flushes 2-3x/year for 3-5 years per log

How: Drill 5/16" holes every 6", tap in plug spawn, seal with cheese wax. Stack logs in shade. Soak 24hr to force fruiting.

🍄 Oyster Mushrooms (Optional)

Location: Under pear trees alongside shiitake logs, or on straw bales

Shade needed: YES — full shade or deep dappled shade

Substrate: Hardwood logs (same as shiitake) or pasteurized straw

When to inoculate: Spring (logs) or anytime (straw, indoors/greenhouse)

Fruits: Faster than shiitake — 3-6 months on logs. Multiple flushes.

How: Same plug-spawn method as shiitake. Blue/grey/pink oyster all work in Zone 7a.

Recommended Placement

  • 🍄 Wine caps: Mulched paths between beds 1-10, around bed 16 (asparagus — already has heavy wood chips)
  • 🍄 Shiitake logs: Stack under pear trees (Bed 17), north side for consistent shade
  • 🍄 Oyster logs: Next to shiitake stack, or try a straw bucket in the greenhouse (Bed 12) for winter fruiting
💡 Tip: Wine caps are the easiest win. You're already putting down 4-6" wood chips on the asparagus bed, around beds, and on 18-2. Just mix in spawn when you mulch in March/April and you'll get mushrooms by September with zero extra work. Shiitake takes more upfront effort (cutting logs, drilling, plugging) but then produces for 3-5 years per log.

📝 Lessons from 4 Years
  • Ongoing Squash bugs are the #1 pest. Blue Hubbard trap crop is the new strategy for 2026. Previous years: hand-picking, neem oil, cardboard traps all help but need to start early. Companion planting (all 3 layers) is the 2026 approach.
  • Ongoing Tomato blight is recurring. Watch closely in July/Aug. Remove affected leaves immediately. Don't overhead water. Good air circulation is key — don't overcrowd bed 1.
  • Ongoing Cilantro bolts fast in heat. Succession planting every 2 weeks is the only way to have continuous supply. Slow-bolt variety (Santo) helps. Stop May-July, resume Aug.
  • Ongoing Asparagus bed gets overrun with weeds. 2026 plan: heavy wood chip mulch (4-6") after spring harvest. No excuses — do it as soon as last harvest is done in May.
  • Ongoing Crabgrass invades 18-2 (Egyptian onions). 2026 plan: cardboard/newspaper base + 4-6" wood chips + plant through holes.
  • Lesson Boston Pickling cucumbers are batch producers — you get a big flush then nothing. Switch to continuous-producing varieties (Spacemaster 80, Diva) for steady supply.
  • Lesson Hot, dry hilltop summers at 650 ft mean deep watering at root level in mornings. Drip or soaker hose beats sprinkler. Mulch everything to retain moisture.
  • Lesson Mono-cropping = pest magnets. 2026 diversifies with companion planting, flowers, herbs, and the 3-layer pest strategy. More variety = fewer problems.
  • Lesson Strawberries & blueberries are keepers. Low maintenance, reliable producers. Keep beds clean and mulched.
  • Lesson Grapes & pears produce well with minimal attention. Just prune and harvest.
  • 2025 Japanese beetles destroyed Catawba grapes. 2026 plan: neem oil spray every 7-10 days Jun–Aug, daily hand-picking mornings into soapy water, milky spore in surrounding ground (Oct). Do NOT use bag traps — they attract more beetles than they catch.
  • 2026 New beds 8, 9, 10 add herbs, cut flowers, and succulents. More beauty, more pollinators, drought-tolerant options. Should make the garden more attractive and resilient.
